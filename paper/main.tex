\address{University of Minnesota, Twin Cities, Minneapolis, MN, USA}

\begin{abstract}
Coordinate models built on a \emph{fixed} finite set of Fourier features---the linear core
of Fourier-feature implicit neural representations---fit signals by least squares on an
exponential dictionary $\Lam$.  We give an identifiability theory for what such models do
to out-of-band content.  (i)~On any subset of a rate-$Q$ sampling grid, an integer tone
congruent to a model frequency modulo $Q$ is \emph{exactly} indistinguishable from that
atom: zero training residual, full-energy reconstruction failure, and---by a two-point
argument---no estimator whatsoever can avoid the error (\emph{silent aliasing}).
(ii)~Random sampling provably destroys this exact aliasing: under timing jitter the tone's
visibility follows the jitter's characteristic function ($v\approx2\pi|r|Q\sigma_t$ for
small Gaussian jitter), and under i.i.d.\ sampling the worst-case aliasability over a
finite candidate set concentrates at $O(\sqrt{m\log(mK/\delta)/N})$.  (iii)~Detection is
dichotomous: an exactly coherent fold is indistinguishable from its in-band twin by
\emph{any} sample-only test (identical observation laws), while visible tones admit
exact noncentral-$\chi^2$ detection power, which calibrated sample-only detectors match
empirically.  Every closed
form is validated against Monte Carlo; trained nonlinear networks (Fourier-feature MLPs,
SIRENs) are studied strictly as empirical extrapolation, with fold patterns predicted by
each network's own NTK linearization and all seed-level failures reported.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
Sampling theory, aliasing, identifiability, Fourier features.
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
Coordinate networks with Fourier features are now standard continuous signal
models~\cite{sitzmann2020siren,tancik2020fourier,mildenhall2020nerf}.  Their analytically
exact core---and the object of every theorem in this paper---is the \emph{fixed
Fourier-feature coordinate model}: a finite frequency set
$\Lam=\{\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m\}\subset\R$ with linear coefficients, fit by least
squares.  Trained nonlinear networks (Fourier-feature MLPs, SIRENs) enter only as
\emph{empirical extrapolation} in Sec.~\ref{sec:exp}, and adaptive or learned frequency
sets are outside the scope of our theory; we do not claim results for ``all INRs''.

\emph{Questions.}  When a signal contains energy outside $\Lam$, three practical
questions arise.  \textbf{Q1}: when is that energy \emph{exactly} invisible in the
samples---fitting perfectly while corrupting the reconstruction?  \textbf{Q2}: how does
the sampling design (grids, jitter, i.i.d.\ draws) change this?  \textbf{Q3}: when can a
practitioner \emph{detect} the problem from the samples alone?

\emph{Contributions.}  (1)~An identifiability framework---sample atoms, equivalence
classes, \emph{visibility} $\vt(\nu)$, \emph{aliasability} $\at(\nu)$---classifying exact
silent aliasing on arbitrary subsets of sampling grids, with an
estimator-independent two-point lower bound (Thm.~\ref{thm:t1}).  (2)~Function-space
accounting: a closed-form continuous Gram matrix with Riesz bounds and an exact error
decomposition keeping coefficient error, aliasing-induced modeled-component error, and
truncation error \emph{separate} (Thm.~\ref{thm:t2}).  (3)~Proof that \emph{random
sampling breaks exact aliasing}, with explicit rates: a characteristic-function law for
jitter and a finite-candidate concentration bound for i.i.d.\ sampling
(Thm.~\ref{thm:t3}).  (4)~A detection dichotomy: impossibility for coherent folds, exact
noncentral-$\chi^2$ power otherwise, and a calibrated empirical operating characteristic
with baselines (Thm.~\ref{thm:t4}, Sec.~\ref{sec:exp}).

\emph{Relation to prior work} (per-item increments; full audit in the repository).
Classical modulo-$N$ aliasing~\cite{shannon1949} is the full-grid special case of
Thm.~\ref{thm:t1}, which covers \emph{arbitrary} grid subsets.  Generalized
sampling~\cite{adcock2016generalized,unser2000sampling} supplies the least-squares recast
we build on; the equivalence-class, sampling-design, and detection results are not part
of it.  The generalized aliasing decomposition of eGAD~\cite{transtrum2024gad} computes an
aliasing operator for \emph{given} samples; our $\at(\nu)$ is a column norm of that
operator, and our new content is probabilistic control of it \emph{over the sampling
design} (Thm.~\ref{thm:t3}).  That randomized timing suppresses aliasing is itself
classical---Balakrishnan derived the characteristic-function attenuation of spectral
replicas under jitter, and Shapiro--Silverman and Beutler established alias-free random
sampling of stochastic processes~\cite{balakrishnan1962jitter,shapiro1960alias,beutler1970alias};
those are asymptotic spectral-replica statements, whereas Thm.~\ref{thm:t3} gives
finite-$N$, high-probability control of visibility and aliasability with respect to a
fixed LS dictionary, uniformly over finite candidate sets.  Basis
mismatch~\cite{chi2011sensitivity} concerns tones
\emph{near} atoms; silent aliasing is the complementary far regime.  The Lomb--Scargle
spectral window~\cite{vanderplas2018lomb} predicts periodogram leakage; we add the
estimator-independent indistinguishability statement and detection theory.  The
structured-dictionary view of INRs~\cite{yuce2022structured} motivates the model class
but contains no sampling theory; INR sampling theorems to
date~\cite{najaf2024sampling,najaf2025superres,saratchandran2024activations} are
achievability results for realizable signals; an NTK bound from missing
frequencies~\cite{ma2025robustifying} gives an error floor but not the fold structure,
invisibility, or detection.  Anti-aliasing INR \emph{architectures}
(band-limited/multiscale designs~\cite{lindell2022bacon,barron2021mipnerf,shabanov2024banf})
prevent folds by construction; we characterize what happens when the features are not so
constrained.  Empirical SIREN aliasing has been
observed~\cite{chandravamsi2025spectral}; we supply theory for the linear core and a
measured, calibrated diagnostic.

\section{Setup and background}\label{sec:setup}
Signals live on $[0,1)$; $e_\omega(x)=e^{i2\pi\omega x}$.  The model class is
$\Mcal_\Lam=\{\sum_k c_k e_{\omega_k}\}$ with $\Lam$ \emph{fixed}.  Samples
$T=\{t_1,\dots,t_N\}\subset[0,1)$ define $S_Tf=(f(t_j))_j$, the synthesis matrix
$\Phimat\in\C^{N\times m}$, $\Phimat_{jk}=e_{\omega_k}(t_j)$, and observations
$\bm y=S_Tf+\bm\varepsilon$.  We write $f=f_{\mathrm{in}}+f_{\mathrm{out}}$ with
$f_{\mathrm{in}}=\sum_k c^\star_ke_{\omega_k}$ and $f_{\mathrm{out}}=\sum_l a_le_{\nu_l}$,
$\nu_l\notin\Lam$.  Real signals are fit with the equivalent real cosine/sine design
(Hermitian symmetry enforced in the parameterization; see the code).  Ordinary least
squares $\hat{\bm c}=\Phimat^\dagger\bm y$ is used throughout the theory (no ridge); the
underdetermined minimum-norm case is treated separately in the code and experiments.

\begin{lemma}[LS stability; standard]\label{lem:ls}
If $\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)>0$ and $f_{\mathrm{out}}=0$ then
$\norm{\hat{\bm c}-\bm c^\star}\le\norm{\bm\varepsilon}/\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)$ and, for
white noise, $\mathbb E\norm{\hat{\bm c}-\bm c^\star}^2=\sigma^2\tr((\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1})$.
\end{lemma}
Textbook LS perturbation, stated for calibration; the average-case bias/variance
decomposition is likewise standard and deferred to the supplement (Prop.~S1).

\section{Main results}\label{sec:main}
\subsection{T1: exact equivalence and silent aliasing}
For $\nu\in\R$ define the \emph{sample atom} $\bm\phi_\nu=(e^{i2\pi\nu t_j})_j$, the
\emph{visibility} and \emph{aliasability}
\begin{equation}
\vt(\nu)=\tfrac{1}{\sqrt N}\norm{(\bm I-\bm P_\Lam)\bm\phi_\nu},\qquad
\at(\nu)=\norm{\Phimat^\dagger\bm\phi_\nu},
\end{equation}
with $\bm P_\Lam$ the orthogonal projector onto $\range\Phimat$.  $\vt(\nu)\in[0,1]$ is
the fraction of the tone's sample energy the model cannot absorb; $\at(\nu)$ is the
coefficient bias a unit tone induces.

\begin{theorem}[Exact equivalence on grid subsets]\label{thm:t1}
Let $\Lam\subset\Z$, let $T$ be \emph{any} subset of the rate-$Q$ grid
$\{0,\dots,Q{-}1\}/Q$ with $\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)>0$ (which requires $N\ge m$ and the
elements of $\Lam$ distinct mod $Q$), and let $\nu\in\Z$.
(i)~$\vt(\nu)=0$ iff $\bm\phi_\nu\in\range\Phimat$, i.e.\ iff a tone at $\nu$ is exactly
reproducible by the model class on the samples.  If $\nu\equiv\omega_k\pmod Q$ then
$\bm\phi_\nu=\bm\phi_{\omega_k}$, hence $\vt(\nu)=0$ \emph{for every such subset} $T$,
the fit of a unit tone is the unit coefficient on atom $k$, and the training residual is
exactly zero.
(ii)~(estimator-independent converse) With $f_1=f_{\mathrm{in}}+a\,e_\nu$ and
$f_2=f_{\mathrm{in}}+a\,e_{\omega_k}$, $\nu\equiv\omega_k\pmod Q$, $\nu\neq\omega_k$:
$S_Tf_1=S_Tf_2$, so under any noise law the two hypotheses induce identical observation
distributions, and \emph{any} estimator $\hat f$ satisfies
$\max_{i\in\{1,2\}}\norm{\hat f-f_i}_{L^2[0,1)}\ge|a|/\sqrt2$.
(iii)~If $\nu\not\equiv\omega_k\pmod Q$ for all $k$ and $[\Phimat\;\bm\phi_\nu]$ has full
column rank, then $\vt(\nu)>0$.
\end{theorem}
\emph{Sketch.}  (i)~$e^{i2\pi\nu g/Q}$ depends on $\nu\bmod Q$; (ii)~is a two-point
(Le Cam) argument with $\norm{e_\nu-e_{\omega_k}}_{L^2}=\sqrt2$; (iii)~is the rank
condition.  Full proofs in the supplement.  \emph{Silent aliasing} is regime (i)--(ii):
the model fits the samples to the noise floor while the reconstruction error is at least
the full out-of-band energy, at any budget $N$ satisfying the rank condition, with $\nu$
arbitrarily far from $\Lam$.  Classical aliasing is the special case $T$ = full grid
($Q=N$)~\cite{shannon1949}; the point here is that \emph{nonuniform designs inherit the
grid's equivalence classes} whenever they live on a grid.

\subsection{T2: function-space accounting}
Coefficient norms are not signal energies unless the dictionary is orthonormal.  Let
$G_{L^2}[k,\ell]=\langle e_{\omega_\ell},e_{\omega_k}\rangle_{L^2[0,1)}
=e^{i\pi\delta}\operatorname{sinc}(\delta)$, $\delta=\omega_\ell-\omega_k$,
$\operatorname{sinc}(x)=\sin(\pi x)/(\pi x)$.

\begin{theorem}[Riesz conversion and exact decomposition]\label{thm:t2}
(i)~With $\lambda_{\min},\lambda_{\max}$ the extreme eigenvalues of $G_{L^2}$ (positive
iff the frequencies are distinct),
$\lambda_{\min}\norm{\bm c}^2\le\norm{\sum_kc_ke_{\omega_k}}_{L^2}^2\le\lambda_{\max}\norm{\bm c}^2$.
(ii)~With $\Delta\bm c=\hat{\bm c}-\bm c^\star$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dec}
\norm{\hat f-f}_{L^2}^2=\Delta\bm c^{*}G_{\Lam\Lam}\Delta\bm c
-2\,\mathrm{Re}\,\Delta\bm c^{*}G_{\Lam,\mathrm{out}}\,\bm a
+\bm a^{*}G_{\mathrm{out}}\,\bm a,
\end{equation}
the three terms being the \emph{modeled-component} error (aliasing bias plus in-band
estimation noise), a cross term (zero for all-integer frequencies), and the
\emph{truncation} energy.
\end{theorem}
Equation~\eqref{eq:dec} is elementary but load-bearing: truncation error exists for
\emph{any} sampling and is not aliasing; only the first term contains the model
\emph{misplacing} energy, and only that term responds to sampling design
(Fig.~\ref{fig:matrix}d).

\subsection{T3: random sampling breaks exact aliasing}
\begin{theorem}[Sampling design]\label{thm:t3}
Let $\Lam\subset\Z$ with $|\Lam|=m$.
(a)~\emph{(jitter)}  Let $t_j=g_j/Q+\eta_j$ with $g_j$ grid points and $\eta_j$ i.i.d.,
mean-zero, with characteristic function $\chi_\eta$, and let $\nu=\omega_k+rQ$,
$r\in\Z\setminus\{0\}$.  For \emph{arbitrary} grid points, the empirical fold coherence
$\frac1N\sum_je^{i2\pi(\nu-\omega_k)t_j}$ has mean $\chi_\eta(2\pi rQ)$ and concentrates
around it at rate $2\sqrt{\log(4/\delta)/N}$.  If moreover the $g_j$ are drawn i.i.d.\
uniformly from the grid (or as a uniformly random subset without replacement) and the
elements of $\Lam$ are distinct modulo $Q$, then
$\vt(\nu)^2=1-|\chi_\eta(2\pi rQ)|^2+O_P\!\big(m\sqrt{\log(m/\delta)/N}\big)$.  For
Gaussian jitter of std $\sigma_t$, $\chi_\eta(2\pi rQ)=e^{-2(\pi rQ\sigma_t)^2}$, giving
the small-jitter law $\vt(\nu)\approx2\pi|r|Q\sigma_t$.
(b)~\emph{(i.i.d.\ samples)}  Let $t_j$ be i.i.d.\ from a density whose limiting
normalized Gram satisfies $G_\infty\succeq\lambda_{\min}\bm I$ and whose limiting
model--candidate coherences vanish on a finite candidate set $\Omega$, $|\Omega|=K$ (for
the uniform density and integer-disjoint candidates, $G_\infty=\bm I$,
$\lambda_{\min}=1$).  With
$\varepsilon=2\sqrt{\log\!\big(4(mK+m^2)/\delta\big)/N}$ and $m\varepsilon<\lambda_{\min}$,
with probability at least $1-\delta$,
\begin{equation}
\max_{\nu\in\Omega}\;\at(\nu)\;\le\;\frac{\sqrt m\,\varepsilon}{\lambda_{\min}-m\varepsilon}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}
\emph{Sketch.}  (a)~On the grid part the fold phase is exactly $1$; the jitter
contributes i.i.d.\ unit-modulus factors, so the coherence is an empirical characteristic
function; Hoeffding on real and imaginary parts.  (b)~Hoeffding + union bound over all
$mK+m^2$ coherences, then a perturbation bound on
$\at(\nu)=\norm{(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat/N)^{-1}(\Phimat^{*}\bm\phi_\nu/N)}$.  Proofs in the
supplement; both laws are verified to tight tolerance in Fig.~\ref{fig:matrix}(a,b).

Three honest distinctions follow: exact grid equivalence persists at every $N$ \emph{if
the design stays on the grid} (Thm.~\ref{thm:t1}); for a fixed tone, randomized designs
kill aliasing at explicit rates---including the per-$N$ \emph{adversarial} candidate
within a finite set (Fig.~\ref{fig:matrix}c); and what no design removes is the
truncation term of~\eqref{eq:dec}, not aliasing.

\subsection{T4: detection---impossibility and power}
\begin{theorem}[Detection dichotomy]\label{thm:t4}
(a)~If $S_Tf_1=S_Tf_2$, then for any noise distribution the observation laws under the
two hypotheses coincide, and every sample-based test has power equal to its size.
(b)~For $\bm y=\bm D\bm\beta+\bm s+\bm\varepsilon$, $\bm\varepsilon\sim\mathcal
N(0,\sigma^2\bm I)$, $\bm D$ the real design of rank $p$ and $\bm s$ the sample vector of
an additive component, the residual statistic
$\norm{(\bm I-\bm P_D)\bm y}^2/\sigma^2$ is $\chi^2_{N-p}$ under H0 and noncentral
$\chi^2_{N-p}(\lambda)$ with $\lambda=\norm{(\bm I-\bm P_D)\bm s}^2/\sigma^2$ under H1:
detection power is driven by \emph{amplitude times visibility}, and is exactly the size
$\alpha$ when $\lambda=0$ (coherent fold).
\end{theorem}
Sample-only detectors must be \emph{calibrated}: thresholds come from an independent
null calibration set and are frozen; test-set FPR/TPR are reported separately.

\emph{Theorem validation (Fig.~\ref{fig:matrix}).}  (a)~the jitter law: empirical
visibility of a grid-coherent tone matches $\sqrt{1-\chi_\eta^2}$ across two decades,
with the exact fold ($v\approx10^{-13}$) at $\sigma_t=0$; (b)~worst-case aliasability
over a $K{=}31$ candidate set under i.i.d.\ sampling decays as $N^{-1/2}$ beneath the
proved bound (loose, as unions are, but finite exactly where the theorem says);
(c)~persistence vs randomization: the grid-subset coherent tone stays exactly invisible
at every $N$, while under i.i.d.\ sampling both the same tone and the per-$N$
\emph{adversarial} candidate are immediately visible; (d)~the decomposition: jitter
drives the aliasing-bias term to the noise level while the truncation floor is untouched
---sampling design removes aliasing, never truncation.

\emph{Detection (Fig.~\ref{fig:roc}).}  With calibration/test separation, sample-only
detectors (ring test, residual, Lomb--Scargle band test, held-out prediction, cross-fit)
track the exact Thm.~\ref{thm:t4}(b) power curve; at the calibrated threshold the
test-set FPR stays at its nominal 5\%.  The indistinguishability demonstration
instantiates Thm.~\ref{thm:t4}(a)'s two-point pair exactly: H1 places a tone at the
grid-coherent $\nu{=}111$, H0 the \emph{same-amplitude} tone at its in-band twin
$\nu-Q=-17$; the grid sample laws are identical, and all five detectors sit at chance
(best-of-five AUC $\NumCohAUC$ with every CI covering $0.5$; best TPR at 5\% FPR
$\NumCohTPR$), at any amplitude by Thm.~\ref{thm:t4}(a).  Against a \emph{no-tone} null, energy-sensitive statistics can
flag that something changed---but cannot attribute it to out-of-band content; it is
this attribution that is impossible.  Reported separately from the operating
characteristics by design.

\emph{Real signals, sample-only.}  On real series (Mauna Loa CO$_2$, sunspots, and nine
2-s speech segments from three Open Speech Repository recordings; anti-aliased
\texttt{resample\_poly} decimation; full records, no window selection), bandwidth
selectors that see \emph{only the training samples} are compared against an oracle that
minimizes the \emph{actual} reconstruction error over the selector grid \emph{and} every
band the selectors chose in that draw---an evaluation-only upper bound that dominates
each selector per draw by construction.  Speech CIs use segment-level $t$-intervals
(the honest replication unit; sampling masks and noise are drawn independently per
segment).
On CO$_2$ the sample-only Lomb--Scargle selector attains RMSE $\NumCoTwoLS$ vs the
oracle's $\NumCoTwoOracle$ (means over 10 draws; paired CIs released)---deployable
selection is not the bottleneck.  On broadband speech \emph{no} selector beats
RMSE${}\approx\NumSpeechOracle$ (oracle $\NumSpeechOracle$, periodogram
$\NumSpeechLS$): at this sampling budget the truncation term of~\eqref{eq:dec}
dominates, the real-data face of the converse.  Cross-validated selection is markedly
less stable under gappy sampling.  Full per-method, per-signal tables with paired 95\%
CIs are in the repository.

\emph{Trained networks (empirical extrapolation only).}  For FF-MLPs, SIRENs, and an
explicitly band-limited head, tone attribution is measured by an ablation (identical
initialization trained with/without the tone) and compared to the prediction of
\emph{each network's own NTK linearization}---no arbitrary reference band.  Across
$\ge20$ sampling seeds per condition, with label-permutation, amplitude, and weight-seed
controls (FF-MLP spot checks, $n{=}5$ each), the linear picture extrapolates well to
\emph{lazy-regime} networks---FF-MLP:
exact fold match $77\%$, top-3 $98\%$, pattern correlation median $0.96$
($n{=}60$); band-limited head: correlation median $0.98$---but \emph{fails} for
SIRENs (exact match $20\%$, correlation median $0.49$, $n{=}20$): trained SIRENs leave
their initialization NTK, and we accordingly make \emph{no} claim that the theory covers
them (Fig.~\ref{fig:nl}).  In
2-D (Fig.~\ref{fig:img}), the linear model reproduces the predicted frequency-vector
fold exactly ((a): planted $(27,3)$ on a rate-32 grid subset folds to $(-5,3)$, residual
$10^{-15}$), and trained 2-D networks show the predicted DFT replicas under
\emph{lattice} (coherent) masks---replica-energy ratio $40{\pm}32$ ($\pm$s.d.) at the
predicted shifts, vs $0.99{\pm}0.09$ under random masks of equal density and $\le0.6$
at matched scale ($3$ images, $12$/$24$ mask draws)---while fitting the observed pixels
to $66$~dB against $12$~dB on held-out pixels (2-D silent aliasing; over-parameterized
scale only).  Ridge and early stopping do \emph{not} remove the
replicas (ratios $89$ and $25$): structured aliasing is a sampling-design property, not
an overfitting artifact; clean/noisy/held-out/full-field PSNR are reported separately in
the released results.

\section{Limitations and conclusion}
Our theorems cover fixed-feature, linear-coefficient Fourier models; trained nonlinear
networks are studied only empirically---and the extrapolation has an empirical boundary:
it holds for lazy-regime FF-MLPs but \emph{fails for SIRENs}, whose training leaves the
initialization NTK.  Adaptive/learned frequency sets are open.  The
concentration bound is a union bound (loose constants); the jitter law's multi-atom
correction is $O_P(m\sqrt{\log/N})$.  Within that scope the theory is complete and
mechanically verified: exact silent aliasing is a property of \emph{grid designs} and is
classified by congruence; randomized sampling provably destroys it at explicit rates;
detectability is dichotomous and quantitatively matched by calibrated sample-only
detectors.  Practical guidance: avoid pure grid designs; jitter of std $\sigma_t$ buys
visibility $\approx2\pi|r|Q\sigma_t$; calibrate diagnostics on explicit nulls; never
read training residual as reconstruction fidelity.
