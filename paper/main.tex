\begin{abstract}
Implicit neural representations (INRs)---SIREN and Fourier-feature coordinate networks---are
increasingly used as continuous models of signals. Recent theory establishes an
\emph{achievability} sampling result: how many samples recover a signal that \emph{is} an INR.
We ask the converse question that governs practice, where signals are \emph{not} INRs: what is
the fundamental limit of representing an arbitrary, noisily and nonuniformly sampled signal with
an INR? We show an INR represents only a \emph{structured} frequency set $\Lam$ fixed by its
features and width/depth, and prove (i) an achievability bound: if the signal spectrum lies in
$\Lam$ and the samples form a frame for $\Lam$, a bandwidth-matched INR recovers it with
closed-form noise gain $\kappa=1/\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)$; (ii) a converse: any out-of-band
energy is \emph{folded} onto $\Lam$ with a sharp aliasing-error floor no width-$W$ INR can beat,
and strikingly the sample fit can be near-perfect while the recovered \emph{spectrum} is
corrupted---\emph{silent aliasing}; and (iii) a statistical minimax version separating an
estimation-variance regime from a non-vanishing aliasing-bias floor. A bandwidth-matched
estimator attains the achievability side and a ground-truth-free diagnostic flags the converse
side. Every theorem is validated against Monte-Carlo simulation, and both sides of the limit are
demonstrated on real audio, scientific and image data. The classical uniform-lowpass folding law
is recovered as a special case; the structured/learned aliasing, silent aliasing, and matched
achievability are new.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
Implicit neural representations, sampling theory, aliasing, generalized sampling, coordinate networks.
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
Implicit neural representations parameterize a signal as a coordinate network
$f_\theta:\R^d\!\to\!\R$ and have become a default continuous model for audio, images, volumes,
and scientific fields \cite{sitzmann2020siren,tancik2020fourier,mildenhall2020nerf}. A large
literature engineers their \emph{architectures}---periodic activations, Fourier features,
band-limited or anti-aliased variants \cite{sitzmann2020siren,tancik2020fourier,shabanov2024banf,barron2021mipnerf}---and
a recent thread gives a \emph{sampling theory}: Najaf et al.\ \cite{najaf2024sampling} and
follow-ups \cite{shi2025superres} prove that a signal which \emph{is} a shallow INR is exactly
recoverable from enough noiseless Fourier measurements. These are all \emph{achievability}
statements about realizable signals.

\emph{The gap.} In practice the signal is not an INR: it is an arbitrary waveform observed at
finitely many, often nonuniform, noisy time samples. Nothing in the above characterizes when the
task is possible, how the answer depends on the network's size, or what failure looks like. We
supply the missing \emph{converse}. The key object is the INR's \emph{representable frequency set}
$\Lam$: for a Fourier-feature/periodic coordinate network the output is a combination of complex
exponentials whose frequencies are integer combinations of the base feature frequencies, a set
that grows with width and depth \cite{yuce2022structured}. A signal is representable only insofar
as its spectrum lies in $\Lam$.

\emph{Contributions.} (1) An achievability bound with a closed-form noise gain tied to the stable
sampling rate of $\Lam$ (Thm.~\ref{thm:ach}). (2) A converse: out-of-band energy folds onto $\Lam$
with a sharp error floor, and a \emph{silent-aliasing} phenomenon in which the sample fit is near
perfect while the recovered spectrum is corrupted (Thm.~\ref{thm:conv}). (3) A statistical minimax
version with a phase transition between a variance regime and a non-vanishing aliasing-bias floor
(Thm.~\ref{thm:stat}). (4) A bandwidth-matched estimator and a ground-truth-free diagnostic, with
Monte-Carlo-validated theory and real-data demonstrations of both sides of the limit.

\emph{Why this is not classical Nyquist.} The representable set $\Lam$ is \emph{not} a lowpass
interval but a structured (and, for adaptive-frequency INRs, signal-dependent) set, so aliasing
folds energy onto a structured target rather than baseband. The uniform-lowpass folding law is one
special case of our converse (Rem.~\ref{rem:classical}); structured/learned aliasing, silent
aliasing, the data-only diagnostic, and matched achievability are new. Our machinery adapts
generalized sampling and stable-sampling-rate ideas \cite{adcock2016generalized,landau1967necessary,unser2000sampling}
to the INR dictionary.

\section{Signal model and representable band}
Let $f:[0,1)\!\to\!\R$ be observed at samples $t_1,\dots,t_N$ (possibly nonuniform) as
$y_j=f(t_j)+\varepsilon_j$. A Fourier-feature INR with feature frequencies inducing the finite set
$\Lam=\{\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m\}\subset\R$ has, in its linear output layer, the model class
$\mathcal M_\Lam=\{\sum_k c_k e^{i2\pi\omega_k x}\}$; deeper/periodic networks enlarge $\Lam$ to
integer combinations of base frequencies \cite{yuce2022structured}. Define the
\emph{synthesis matrix} $\Phimat\in\C^{N\times m}$, $\Phimat_{jk}=e^{i2\pi\omega_k t_j}$; fitting
the INR to the samples is the (ridge) least-squares problem
$\hat{\bm c}=\arg\min_{\bm c}\norm{\bm y-\Phimat\bm c}^2$. Write the signal as
$f=f_{\mathrm{in}}+f_{\mathrm{out}}$ with $\supp(\hat f_{\mathrm{in}})\subseteq\Lam$ and
$f_{\mathrm{out}}$ spectrally disjoint from $\Lam$, so
$\bm y=\Phimat\bm c^\star+\bm g+\bm\varepsilon$, $g_j=f_{\mathrm{out}}(t_j)$. The lower/upper
\emph{frame bounds} of the sampled dictionary are $A=\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)^2$,
$B=\sigma_{\max}(\Phimat)^2$; $A>0$ iff the samples determine $\mathcal M_\Lam$.

\section{Fundamental limits}
\begin{theorem}[Achievability]\label{thm:ach}
If $f_{\mathrm{out}}=0$ and $\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)>0$, then
$\norm{\hat{\bm c}-\bm c^\star}\le\kappa\,\norm{\bm\varepsilon}$ with noise gain
$\kappa=\norm{(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}}_2=1/\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)$, and for white
noise of variance $\sigma^2$, $\ \mathbb E\norm{\hat{\bm c}-\bm c^\star}^2=\sigma^2\tr\!\big((\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\big)$.
\end{theorem}
The condition $A>0$ holds once the sampling density exceeds the stable rate of $\Lam$; for
$\Lam\subseteq[-W,W]$ this is the Landau density $N\gtrsim 2W$ \cite{landau1967necessary}.

\begin{theorem}[Converse and aliasing]\label{thm:conv}
Let $P=\Phimat(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}$. With $f_{\mathrm{out}}\neq0$ the least-squares
estimate is biased, $\hat{\bm c}=\bm c^\star+\Delta\bm c$,
$\Delta\bm c=(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}\bm g$, and:
(i) no INR in $\mathcal M_\Lam$ fits the noiseless samples better than
$\min_{\bm c}\norm{\bm y-\Phimat\bm c}=\norm{(\bm I-P)\bm g}$;
(ii) the recovered spectrum is corrupted by $\Delta\bm c$, and $\norm{\Delta\bm c}$ can be
$\Theta(\norm{f_{\mathrm{out}}})$ even when $\norm{(\bm I-P)\bm g}\!\to\!0$ (\emph{silent aliasing});
(iii) a tone $e_\nu$ folds onto $\omega_{k^\star}\in\Lam$, $k^\star=\arg\max_k|[\Delta\bm c(\nu)]_k|$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:classical}
For uniform $t_j=j/N$ and $\Lam$ an integer lowpass band, part (iii) reduces to
$\nu\bmod N$: classical aliasing is the coherent special case.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Statistical]\label{thm:stat}
For random $\bm c^\star$, out-of-band coefficients of power $\bm p$, and white noise $\sigma^2$, the
Bayes risk decomposes as an estimation-variance term $\sigma^2\tr((\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1})$,
which vanishes as $N\!\to\!\infty$, plus an aliasing-bias floor
$\tr(\bm M\bm\Sigma_g\bm M^{*})$ with $\bm M=(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}$ and
$\bm\Sigma_g=\Phimat_{\mathrm{out}}\mathrm{diag}(\bm p)\Phimat_{\mathrm{out}}^{*}$, which does
\emph{not} vanish under coherent aliasing. A sharp threshold in $(W,N,\text{spillover})$ separates
the two regimes.
\end{theorem}
Proofs are elementary least-squares/projection arguments; each identity is asserted against
Monte-Carlo in the released test suite.

\section{Matched estimator and diagnostic}
\emph{Bandwidth matching.} Estimating the essential band from the samples via a nonuniform
periodogram and choosing $\Lam$ to cover it places the signal in the Thm.~\ref{thm:ach} regime; this
is the estimator we use. \emph{Diagnostic.} Because a coherent fold is invisible in-sample
(Thm.~\ref{thm:conv}(ii)), we test band-limitation to $\Lam$ by fitting an enlarged dictionary
$\Lam\cup\Lam_{\mathrm{ring}}$ and measuring the recovered energy on the ring; significant ring
energy flags out-of-band content without any ground truth.

\section{Experiments}
All theory is validated against Monte-Carlo (frame bounds, $\kappa$, aliasing floor, fold law,
statistical regimes). Fig.~\ref{fig:silent} shows silent aliasing: the fixed-feature INR fits the
samples to the noise floor yet plants a spurious spectral peak at exactly the predicted fold
frequency. Fig.~\ref{fig:phase} shows the phase transition: for an in-band signal the error decays
to the noise floor as sampling density grows (achievability), while any out-of-band content pins
the error at a floor independent of density (converse).

\emph{Real data, both sides} (Fig.~\ref{fig:real}). We sweep the INR bandwidth $B_{\mathrm{INR}}$ on
real signals and read the reconstruction-error knee, the empirical learned-Nyquist rate. Mauna~Loa
$\mathrm{CO_2}$ (NOAA) is bandlimited (annual cycle), giving a \emph{sharp knee} at its spectral
peak: a narrow INR aliases it, a matched INR recovers it (RMSE $0.42\!\to\!0.16$). Speech (Open
Speech Repository) is broadband, so the error descends in a staircase and never reaches the noise
floor---the aliasing side---even at the widest practical width. Smoothed sunspots (SILSO) behave
like $\mathrm{CO_2}$. The phenomenon persists for \emph{trained nonlinear} INRs: a shallow
Fourier-feature network fits undersampled data but folds an out-of-hull tone in exactly as
predicted, and a two-dimensional INR on sparse image pixels exhibits the same limit---an
over-scaled band yields high sample-PSNR but a moir\'e-corrupted field (2-D silent aliasing), a
matched band recovers detail.

\section{Conclusion}
INRs alias, and provably so: they represent only a structured band $\Lam$, out-of-band energy folds
onto $\Lam$ with an error floor, and the corruption can be spectrally severe yet invisible in the
sample fit. The limit is sharp, matched by a bandwidth-selecting estimator, detectable from data,
and visible on real audio, scientific and image signals. Beyond the fixed-feature core, deriving
$\Lam$ for general depth and adaptive-frequency INRs, and converses under the exact training
dynamics, are natural next steps.
