\begin{abstract}
Implicit neural representations (INRs)---SIREN and Fourier-feature coordinate networks---are
increasingly used as continuous models of signals. A recent \emph{achievability} sampling theory
addresses signals that are themselves INRs; we address the \emph{converse} question that governs
practice, where the signal is arbitrary and observed at finite, noisy, nonuniform samples. A
fixed-feature INR (fixed feature frequencies, linear output layer) represents only a
\emph{structured} frequency set $\Lam$. Casting its least-squares fit as generalized sampling on
this dictionary, we prove: (i) achievability---if the signal spectrum lies in $\Lam$ and the
samples give $\Lam$ full column rank, a bandwidth-matched INR recovers it with closed-form noise
gain $\kappa=1/\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)$; (ii) a converse---out-of-band energy is \emph{folded} onto
$\Lam$ with an error floor no fixed-feature INR can beat, and the sample residual can be far
smaller than the resulting spectral corruption (\emph{silent aliasing}), with a \emph{non-classical}
fold target when $\Lam$ is structured; (iii) an average-case decomposition of the estimator's error
into an estimation-variance term and a coherence-dependent aliasing-variance floor. We give a
bandwidth-matched estimator and a ground-truth-free diagnostic, validate every identity against
Monte-Carlo, and demonstrate both sides of the limit on real audio and scientific signals---the
phenomenon persisting for trained \emph{nonlinear} and 2-D INRs. The classical uniform-lowpass fold
is one special case; the structured/learned aliasing, the named silent-aliasing regime, and the
diagnostic are new.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
Implicit neural representations, sampling theory, aliasing, generalized sampling, coordinate networks.
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
Implicit neural representations parameterize a signal as a coordinate network
$f_\theta:\R^d\!\to\!\R$ and are now a default continuous model for audio, images, volumes, and
scientific fields \cite{sitzmann2020siren,tancik2020fourier,mildenhall2020nerf}. A large literature
engineers their \emph{architectures}---periodic activations, Fourier features, band-limited and
anti-aliased variants \cite{sitzmann2020siren,tancik2020fourier,shabanov2024banf,barron2021mipnerf,saratchandran2024activations}---and
a recent thread gives a \emph{sampling theory}: Najaf and Ongie \cite{najaf2024sampling} and the
super-resolution follow-up \cite{najaf2025superres} prove that a signal which \emph{is} a shallow
INR is exactly recoverable from enough noiseless Fourier measurements. These are \emph{achievability}
statements about realizable signals \cite{jayasundara2026survey}.

\emph{The gap.} In practice the signal is not an INR: it is an arbitrary waveform at finitely many,
often nonuniform, noisy samples. Nothing above says when the task is possible, how the answer
depends on the network's capacity, or what failure looks like. We supply the converse. The key
object is the INR's \emph{representable frequency set} $\Lam$: for a Fourier-feature/periodic
coordinate network the output is a combination of complex exponentials whose frequencies are integer
combinations of the base feature frequencies---a set that grows with width and depth
\cite{yuce2022structured}. A signal is representable only insofar as its spectrum lies in $\Lam$.

\emph{Scope and contributions.} Our theorems concern the analytically exact case: a fixed-feature
INR whose output layer is linear over $\Lam$. Its least-squares fit is generalized (nonharmonic)
sampling on the finite dictionary $\Lam$ \cite{adcock2016generalized,unser2000sampling}; that recast
is the mechanism. On top of it we establish the INR-specific facts: (1) an achievability bound with
a closed-form noise gain (Thm.~\ref{thm:ach}); (2) a converse in which out-of-band energy folds onto
$\Lam$ with an error floor, producing \emph{silent aliasing}---small sample residual, large spectral
corruption---and, for structured $\Lam$, a fold target unlike the classical lowpass law
(Thm.~\ref{thm:conv}); (3) an average-case error decomposition separating estimation variance from a
coherence-dependent aliasing-variance floor (Thm.~\ref{thm:stat}); and (4) a bandwidth-matched
estimator, a ground-truth-free diagnostic, Monte-Carlo-validated theory, and real-data
demonstrations of both sides, which persist empirically for trained nonlinear and 2-D INRs.

\emph{Relation to Nyquist.} When $\Lam=\{-B,\dots,B\}$ and sampling is uniform, our converse reduces
to classical aliasing (Rem.~\ref{rem:classical}). The novelty is that $\Lam$ is a \emph{structured}
(and, for adaptive-frequency INRs, signal-dependent) set, so energy folds onto a structured target;
the named silent-aliasing regime, the data-only diagnostic, and the matched estimator have no
lowpass analogue.

\section{Signal model and representable band}
Let $f:[0,1)\!\to\!\R$ be observed at samples $t_1,\dots,t_N$ (possibly nonuniform) as
$y_j=f(t_j)+\varepsilon_j$. A fixed-feature INR with feature set inducing
$\Lam=\{\omega_1,\dots,\omega_m\}\subset\R$ has model class
$\Mcal_\Lam=\{\sum_k c_k e_{\omega_k}\}$ where $e_\omega(x)=e^{i2\pi\omega x}$; deeper/periodic
networks enlarge $\Lam$ to integer combinations of base frequencies \cite{yuce2022structured}. Define
the \emph{synthesis matrix} $\Phimat\in\C^{N\times m}$, $\Phimat_{jk}=e_{\omega_k}(t_j)$; fitting the
INR is the (ridge) least-squares problem $\hat{\bm c}=\arg\min_{\bm c}\norm{\bm y-\Phimat\bm c}^2$.
Split $f=f_{\mathrm{in}}+f_{\mathrm{out}}$ with $\supp(\hat f_{\mathrm{in}})\subseteq\Lam$ and
$f_{\mathrm{out}}$ spectrally disjoint from $\Lam$ (frequencies $\nu_l$, synthesis matrix
$\Phimat_{\mathrm{out}}$), so $\bm y=\Phimat\bm c^\star+\bm g+\bm\varepsilon$ with
$g_j=f_{\mathrm{out}}(t_j)$. The lower/upper \emph{frame bounds} are $A=\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)^2$,
$B_{\!f}=\sigma_{\max}(\Phimat)^2$; $A>0$ iff $\Phimat$ has full column rank ($m\le N$, generic
positions), i.e.\ the samples determine $\Mcal_\Lam$.

\section{Fundamental limits}
\begin{theorem}[Achievability]\label{thm:ach}
If $f_{\mathrm{out}}=0$ and $\Phimat$ has full column rank ($\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)>0$), then
$\norm{\hat{\bm c}-\bm c^\star}\le\kappa\,\norm{\bm\varepsilon}$ with noise gain
$\kappa=\norm{(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}}_2=1/\sigma_{\min}(\Phimat)$, and for white noise
of variance $\sigma^2$, $\mathbb E\norm{\hat{\bm c}-\bm c^\star}^2=\sigma^2\tr((\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1})$.
\end{theorem}
Full column rank needs $N\ge m=|\Lam|$; for the special case $\Lam=\{-B,\dots,B\}$ this coincides
with the Landau density $N\gtrsim 2B$ \cite{landau1967necessary}, but for a sparse structured $\Lam$
the binding requirement is $N\ge|\Lam|$, not the band extent.

\begin{theorem}[Converse and aliasing]\label{thm:conv}
Let $P=\Phimat(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}$. With $f_{\mathrm{out}}\neq0$ the estimate is
biased, $\hat{\bm c}=\bm c^\star+\Delta\bm c$, $\Delta\bm c=(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}\bm g$,
and: (i) no INR in $\Mcal_\Lam$ fits the noiseless samples better than
$\min_{\bm c}\norm{\bm y-\Phimat\bm c}=\norm{(\bm I-P)\bm g}$; (ii) the recovered spectrum is
corrupted by $\Delta\bm c$, and as an out-of-band tone $e_\nu$ approaches an atom
($\nu\!\to\!\omega_k$) the residual $\norm{(\bm I-P)\bm g}\!\to\!0$ while $\norm{\Delta\bm c}\!\to\!|a|$
(\emph{silent aliasing}: the fit understates the corruption); (iii) the fold target is
$\omega_{k^\star}$, $k^\star=\arg\max_k|[\Delta\bm c(\nu)]_k|$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}\label{rem:classical}
For uniform $t_j=j/N$ and $\Lam$ an integer lowpass band, (iii) reduces to $\nu\bmod N$: classical
aliasing is the coherent special case. For structured $\Lam$ and nonuniform samples the fold target
is non-classical (Fig.~\ref{fig:silent}).
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Average-case decomposition]\label{thm:stat}
Under a zero-mean prior on $\bm c^\star$, out-of-band coefficients of power $\bm p$, and white noise
$\sigma^2$, the mean-squared error of the least-squares estimator is
$\sigma^2\tr((\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1})+\tr(\bm M\bm\Sigma_g\bm M^{*})$, with
$\bm M=(\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1}\Phimat^{*}$ and
$\bm\Sigma_g=\Phimat_{\mathrm{out}}\mathrm{diag}(\bm p)\Phimat_{\mathrm{out}}^{*}$. The
estimation-variance term vanishes as the sampling density grows; the aliasing-variance term persists
when the out-of-band content is \emph{commensurate} with the sampling (coherent fold) and otherwise
decays as $O(1/N^2)$. Their crossover $N^\star$ solves
$\sigma^2\tr((\Phimat^{*}\Phimat)^{-1})=\tr(\bm M\bm\Sigma_g\bm M^{*})$.
\end{theorem}
The proofs are elementary least-squares/projection identities; each is asserted against Monte-Carlo
in the released test suite.

\section{Matched estimator and diagnostic}
\emph{Bandwidth matching.} Estimating the essential band from the samples via a nonuniform
periodogram and choosing $\Lam$ to cover it places the signal in the Thm.~\ref{thm:ach} regime; this
is the estimator we use. \emph{Diagnostic.} Because a coherent fold is nearly invisible in-sample
(Thm.~\ref{thm:conv}(ii)), we test band-limitation to $\Lam$ by fitting an enlarged dictionary
$\Lam\cup\Lam_{\mathrm{ring}}$ ($\Lam_{\mathrm{ring}}$ a ring of candidate frequencies outside
$\Lam$) and measuring the recovered energy on the ring; significant ring energy flags out-of-band
content with no ground truth.

\section{Experiments}
Every identity is validated against Monte-Carlo (frame bounds, $\kappa$, aliasing floor, fold law,
the Thm.~\ref{thm:stat} terms). Fig.~\ref{fig:silent} shows the structured, nonuniform silent-aliasing
regime: the fixed-feature INR fits the samples to near the noise floor yet plants a spurious peak at
the non-classical fold frequency, and the ground-truth-free diagnostic flags it. Fig.~\ref{fig:phase}
(a $40$-trial average) shows the phase transition: an in-band signal's error decays toward the noise
floor as sampling density grows (achievability), while any out-of-band content pins the error at a
floor independent of density (converse; the density-independent height is the unrepresentable energy
$\norm{f_{\mathrm{out}}}$).

\emph{Real data, both sides} (Fig.~\ref{fig:real}). Sweeping the INR bandwidth $B_{\mathrm{INR}}$ on a
real signal and reading the reconstruction-error knee gives the empirical learned-Nyquist rate. Mauna
Loa $\mathrm{CO_2}$ (NOAA), after linear detrending, is bandlimited (annual cycle), giving a
\emph{sharp knee} at its spectral peak: a narrow INR aliases it, a matched INR recovers it
($\mathrm{RMSE}\ 0.41\!\to\!0.16$). Broadband speech (Open Speech Repository) has no clean knee and the
error never reaches the noise floor within any practical width---the aliasing side. Smoothed sunspots
(SILSO; the smoothing is a documented lowpass proxy) behave like $\mathrm{CO_2}$. The phenomenon
persists for trained \emph{nonlinear} INRs---a shallow Fourier-feature network fits undersampled data
but folds an out-of-hull tone in as the linear theory predicts (and a bandwidth-covering network
recovers the signal, matching the linear estimator)---and for a 2-D INR on sparse image pixels
(Fig.~\ref{fig:image}): an \emph{over-scaled} representable band silently aliases (high sample-PSNR
$\approx50$~dB but a moir\'e-corrupted field, $16$~dB), while a band matched to the sampling is safe.
The single-realization figures are illustrative; the phase transition is the averaged result.

\section{Conclusion}
Fixed-feature INRs alias, and provably so: they represent only a structured band $\Lam$, out-of-band
energy folds onto $\Lam$ with an error floor, and the corruption can be spectrally severe yet nearly
invisible in the sample fit. The limit is sharp, matched by a bandwidth-selecting estimator,
detectable from data, and visible on real signals, persisting empirically for trained nonlinear and
2-D INRs. Extending the converse to adaptive-frequency $\Lam$ (where the network can move atoms onto
the signal) and to the exact training dynamics are the natural next steps.
